An Ancient, Unseen Map

For millennia, countless species have navigated epic journeys across continents and oceans. From the pole-to-pole flight of the Arctic Tern to the trans-oceanic voyage of the Blackpoll Warbler, these migrations rely on an incredible array of natural cues. One of the most vital is the celestial compass: an innate ability to read the map of the night sky—the stars, the moon, and the Milky Way—to guide their way.

How Do They Navigate? A Multi-Modal System

Animals don't rely on a single tool. Their navigation is a sophisticated, redundant system integrating multiple environmental cues. This ensures they can find their way even when one signal, like the stars on a cloudy night, is unavailable.

A Growing Shadow

This ancient navigational system faces a modern, pervasive threat: Artificial Light at Night (ALAN). This form of pollution is rapidly erasing the night sky, creating a disorienting glare that has profound consequences for migratory wildlife.

The Fatal Attraction

Artificial lights create "ecological traps." They attract and disorient animals, leading to deadly consequences that ripple through ecosystems.

Charting a Brighter, Darker Future

Practical Mitigation Strategies

  • 🛥
    Shield Lights: Ensure all light is directed downwards where it's needed, preventing upward glare.
  • 🔦
    Use Warmer Colors: Choose amber, orange, or red lights, which are less disruptive to wildlife than harsh blue/white lights.
  • 🕑
    Install Timers & Sensors: Use light only when and where it's necessary to reduce overall usage.
  • ❌
    Just Turn Them Off: The simplest solution. Turn off non-essential lights, especially during peak migration seasons.
